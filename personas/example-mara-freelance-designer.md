# Persona 01: Mara, freelance designer in late-payment limbo

> **Grounding status:** `demo (fictional)`
> **Grounded in:** Nobody. This is a worked example showing the level of specificity required.
> **Last validated against a real participant:** never, and never will be

⚠️ **This persona is fiction.** It exists to show what "grounded" looks like structurally:
specific episodes with dates, amounts, and emotional beats; coping mechanisms; a voice. Your
real personas must draw this texture from real people (yourself, customers, support tickets,
sales calls). Shipping a fictional persona as if it were grounded defeats the entire skill.

---

## 1. Situation

- **Role / occupation:** Freelance brand designer (logos, identity systems, pitch decks)
- **Age:** 34
- **Time in this situation:** 3 years freelancing after 7 years in agencies
- **Location / environment:** Mid-size city, works from home, clients mostly remote
- **Relevant skill level, real:** Excellent designer, weak at money operations. Invoices from
  a template, no contract on about half her projects. **/ claimed:** "I run a studio."
- **Status / constraints:** Sole trader; no retainer clients yet, all project work
- **Household / team:** Partner with a salaried job (the safety net she resents needing)
- **Work & income:** 4-7 projects a quarter; good quarters cover bad ones, barely
- **Money stress level:** Managing, with acute spikes whenever two invoices go long at once

## 2. Scar tissue

- **The 87-day agency invoice:** Her biggest client, a design agency that subcontracts overflow
  to her, paid an $8,000 invoice 87 days late last spring. She sent two "just checking in :)"
  emails over those three months and hated herself for the smiley both times. The agency never
  apologized; the project manager just said "finance batches these." She still takes their work.
- **The $4,200 write-off:** A startup founder ghosted after delivery of a full identity system.
  Two emails, one call attempt, then nothing. She never sent a third email because "what's the
  move after that, a lawyer? Over four grand?" Wrote it off, told nobody except her partner,
  who said "you need contracts," which ended the conversation.
- **The deposit that saved her:** One client tried to cancel mid-project; because this one time
  she'd taken a 40% deposit, she kept it. She knows deposits work. She still doesn't always ask,
  because asking feels like accusing a new client of being a deadbeat before the work starts.

## 3. Current coping mechanisms

- A spreadsheet tab named "WAITING" she opens about twice a week and closes quickly
- The "just checking in :)" email, never escalating past friendly
- Prices new projects 10-15% higher for clients who "feel slow" (pure vibes, no data)
- Vents in a freelancer Discord; has absorbed the folk rule "never work without a deposit"
  without consistently applying it

## 4. Psychology

- **Anxiety profile:** The gap between sending an invoice and hearing anything. Not the work,
  the silence. Checking her banking app has become a tic.
- **Posture toward the counterparty:** Conflict-avoidant with clients she wants to keep;
  privately furious. The fury goes into the Discord, not the email.
- **Trust threshold:** Would let software *draft* a chasing email; would not let it *send*
  unseen. "If it sends something pushy to the agency, that's my biggest client gone." Instant
  trust-breaker: any tool that contacts a client without showing her first.
- **Avoidance triggers:** Anything that makes the chase feel legal or formal. The word
  "demand letter" makes her physically wince.
- **Self-image:** A real designer who is one rude email away from being seen as "difficult."
  Believes agencies pay late because they can, and that she has no leverage. `[ASSUMED: confirm
  whether the no-leverage belief survives her first retainer client]`

## 5. Voice & speech

- **Register & vocabulary:** Casual, self-deprecating, precise about design, vague about money
  ("like four grand? I think? I stopped looking")
- **Jargon habits:** Fluent in design jargon, avoids financial terms; says "the money stuff"
- **Emotional tells:** Jokes when it gets uncomfortable; goes quiet when the write-off comes up;
  heats up about the agency ("EIGHTY-SEVEN days")
- **Sample line, verbatim-style:** "I spent four years learning typography and now my actual
  job is writing 'just bumping this!' emails to people who owe me money."

## 6. Relationship to the jobs-to-be-done

| Job | Reaction | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Track every outstanding invoice automatically | eager | replaces the WAITING tab she avoids |
| Draft escalating chase emails in her voice | eager, with veto | must sound like her, must not embarrass her |
| Send chasers automatically on a schedule | skeptical | trust-breaker if unseen; see §4 |
| Flag when a client's pattern predicts non-payment | curious | "I do this on vibes already" |

## 7. Interview behaviour

- **Verbosity:** Storyteller once warm; opens guarded on money specifics
- **Guardedness:** Will discuss lateness freely; the write-off needs the 5 Whys and a pause
- **What they'll volunteer vs. what needs probing:** Volunteers agency war stories; needs
  probing on what she's actually tried (short list, embarrassing to her)
- **Where they'd surprise you (within character):** Defends her late-paying agency client when
  the interviewer criticizes them ("they're not evil, they're disorganized")
